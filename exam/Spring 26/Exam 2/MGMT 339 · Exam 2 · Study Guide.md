## Process Strategy & Analysis

### **What Is a Process?**

- **Definition & Strategic Role**
    - What is a process? (A sequence of activities that transforms inputs into outputs delivering value to a customer.)
    - Why are process decisions considered strategic? (They must align with long-term competitive goals across quality, flexibility, time, and cost.)
    - What happens when processes are faulty? (Competitive advantage is lost — no amount of good intention fixes a broken process.)

### **Process Divergence & Process Flow**

- **Two Structural Characteristics**
    
    - What is **Process Divergence**? (The degree to which tasks are customized, with latitude in how they are performed.)
    - What is **Process Flow**? (How work is organized and sequenced — flexible or line.)
    - What is the relationship between them? (More customization → higher divergence → more flexible flow. They always move together.)
- **High vs. Low**
    
    |Characteristic|High End|Low End|
    |---|---|---|
    |**Process Divergence**|Flexible, unique paths for each customer|Standardized tasks, same sequence every time|
    |**Process Flow**|Tasks can occur in many different orders|Fixed, repeatable sequence|
    

### **Process Structure in Services — The Customer-Contact Matrix**

- **What It Classifies**
    
    - What two dimensions define a service process on this matrix? (Degree of customer contact and level of customization.)
- **Three Service Types**
    
    - **Front Office:** What are the characteristics? (High interaction, highly customized, flexible flows, individualized processes. Examples: consulting, law, architecture.)
    - **Hybrid Office:** What are the characteristics? (Some interaction, standard service with options, flexible flows with dominant paths. Examples: hospital, bank branch.)
    - **Back Office:** What are the characteristics? (Low interaction, standardized, routine line flows. Examples: check processing, online transactions.)
- **Strategic Fit**
    
    - What does "strategic fit" mean here? (The level of customer contact and customization must match the process design — misalignment leads to either excessive cost or disappointed customers.)

### **Process Structure in Manufacturing — The Product-Process Matrix**

- **What It Classifies**
    
    - What two dimensions define a manufacturing process on this matrix? (Volume of output and degree of product customization.)
- **Four Manufacturing Process Types**
    
    - **Job Process:** What are the characteristics? (Low volume, made-to-order, highly customized, flexible tasks. Examples: custom furniture, specialized equipment.)
    - **Batch Process:** What are the characteristics? (Multiple products, low–moderate volume, disconnected line flows, moderately repetitive. Examples: bakeries, craft breweries, clothing.)
    - **Line Process:** What are the characteristics? (Few major products, higher volume, connected line flows, highly repetitive. Examples: automobile assembly, fast food.)
    - **Continuous Flow Process:** What are the characteristics? (High volume, standardized commodity products, continuous flows, little flexibility. Examples: oil refining, chemicals, paper mills.)

### **The Combined Matrix**

- **Mapping Services and Manufacturing Together**
    - How do service and manufacturing process types relate? (They map onto the same customization spectrum — Job/Front Office at one extreme, Continuous Flow/Back Office at the other.)
    - What is the danger of being "off the diagonal"? (High customization with line-process infrastructure creates excessive cost; high standardization with job-process economics disappoints customers.)

### **The Three Strategic Levers**

These three decisions always move together as you slide across the matrix.

- **Customer Involvement**
    
    - What does it measure? (How much the customer participates in shaping the process and output.)
    - High involvement examples: IKEA self-assembly, ATMs, self-checkout.
    - What are the advantages? (Can lower cost, increase speed, improve personalization.)
    - What are the disadvantages? (Introduces variability; requires stronger interpersonal skills.)
- **Resource Flexibility**
    
    - What does it measure? (How much workers and equipment can handle varied tasks.)
    - What is the trade-off? (Flexibility costs efficiency — the more you can do, the less optimized you are at any one thing.)
    - How does flexibility change across the matrix? (High customization → cross-trained generalists and general-purpose equipment. Low customization → specialized roles and dedicated machines.)
- **Capital Intensity**
    
    - What does it measure? (The mix of automation vs. labor in the process.)
    - **Fixed automation:** What is it? (Efficient at high volume for one product — assembly lines, refineries.)
    - **Flexible automation:** What is it? (Programmable and adaptable to multiple products — robots, CNC machines, 3D printing.)
    - What is the key constraint? (High investment requires high volume to justify — you cannot run a $10M automated line for 500 units per year.)
- **How All Three Move Together**
    
    |Lever|High Customization|Low Customization|
    |---|---|---|
    |**Customer Involvement**|High — customer shapes the output|Low — customer just receives it|
    |**Resource Flexibility**|High — generalist workers & equipment|Low — specialized roles & machines|
    |**Capital Intensity**|Low — labor-driven|High — automation-driven|
    

### **Production & Inventory Strategies**

- **The Four Strategies**
    
    |Strategy|What's Stocked|Engineering|Lead Time|Typical Process|
    |---|---|---|---|---|
    |**DTO** (Design-to-Order)|Nothing|Full custom design|Longest|Job|
    |**MTO** (Make-to-Order)|Nothing|Minor adaptation|Long|Job / Small Batch|
    |**ATO** (Assemble-to-Order)|Subassemblies & components|Configuration only|Short|Batch + Line|
    |**MTS** (Make-to-Stock)|Finished goods ready to ship|Complete — no changes|Immediate|Line / Continuous|
    
- ***Think about it***
    
    - As customization decreases along this spectrum, what changes? (Less engineering per order, inventory moves earlier in the process, lead time shortens, customer involvement decreases.)
    - How does the customer involvement shift as you move from DTO to MTS?

### **Manufacturing vs. Services — The Blurred Line**

- **Servitization**
    
    - What is servitization? (When manufacturers wrap services around their products to create recurring revenue and switching costs.)
    - Examples: Apple (hardware + App Store/iCloud), Tesla (cars + software subscriptions), Rolls-Royce "Power by the Hour" (thrust per flight hour, not the engine), John Deere (precision agriculture data subscriptions).
- **Services Adopting Manufacturing Discipline**
    
    - What does this look like? (Applying standardized, repeatable processes to service delivery at scale.)
    - Example: Starbucks — standardized recipes, centralized roasting, automated espresso machines.

### **Strategies for Change**

- **Two Complementary Approaches**
    - **Process Reengineering:** What is it? (Radical redesign starting from scratch to achieve breakthrough improvements.)
    - **Process Improvement:** What is it? (Ongoing, incremental changes to make existing processes better.)
    - What do both require? (Process analysis to identify waste, delays, safety issues, and customer impact.)

### **Process Analysis Tools**

- **Productivity**
    
    - What is productivity? (Value of outputs produced divided by the value of input resources.)
    - **Labor Productivity:** Output ÷ Labor hours (or workers).
    - **Multifactor Productivity (MFP):** Output value ÷ combined dollar cost of all inputs (labor + materials + overhead). Why convert inputs to dollars? (To compare inputs in a common unit when they differ in type.)
- **Flowcharts**
    
    - What is the purpose? (Trace the flow of information, materials, or customers through process steps; visualize sequencing, decisions, and divergence points.)
    - **Swim Lane Flowchart:** What does it add? (Maps the process across departments; each "lane" is a functional area. Highlights handoffs between departments — where errors and delays often occur.)
    - **Service Blueprint:** What is it? (A special flowchart for services that uses a line of visibility to separate front office steps — visible to the customer — from back office steps.)
- **Process Charts**
    
    - What do they document? (Each activity in a process, categorized by type.)
    
    |Symbol|Activity Type|Description|
    |---|---|---|
    |**Operation**|Changes or creates something|(e.g., drilling, serving a customer)|
    |**Transportation**|Moves something|(e.g., materials, people, tools)|
    |**Inspection**|Checks/validates without changing|(e.g., quality check)|
    |**Delay**|Waiting or idle time|(e.g., queue, waiting for approval)|
    |**Storage**|Items put away for later|(e.g., inventory, filing)|
    
    - What is the value of categorizing activities this way? (Separates value-adding steps from non-value-adding ones, making improvement opportunities visible.)
- **Work Measurement Techniques**
    
    - Why estimate task times? (Needed for capacity planning, scheduling, performance appraisal, and constraint management.)
    - **Time Study:** Trained analyst directly times tasks; best for stable, repetitive work.
    - **Work Sampling:** Random observations to estimate time across activity types; good for flexible, divergent processes.
    - **Learning Curve Analysis:** Captures how time per unit decreases as cumulative output doubles; useful for new products or processes. What is the learning rate? (The percentage by which average time per unit falls each time output doubles.)
- **Data Analysis Tools**
    
    - **Histogram:** Shows the frequency distribution of data — useful for seeing spread, averages, and variation.
    - **Scatter Diagram:** Plots two variables to reveal whether a correlation exists between them.
    - **Cause & Effect (Fishbone) Diagram:** Relates a key performance problem to its potential causes across categories (Methods, People, Equipment, etc.). When should you use it? (To diagnose root causes before jumping to solutions.)
    - **Pareto Chart:** Bar chart ranking causes by frequency with a cumulative percentage line. What rule underlies it? (The 80/20 rule — roughly 80% of problems come from 20% of causes.) When should you use it? (To prioritize which problems to fix first.)

### **Redesigning & Managing Process Improvements**

- **Questioning & Brainstorming**
    
    - What is the 6W+H approach? (Ask What, When, Who, Where, How, How well — and challenge necessity with Why — for each step.)
    - Why involve frontline staff? (The people doing the work know things no report captures.)
- **Benchmarking**
    
    - What is benchmarking? (Comparing your process against a best-in-class operation to set improvement goals.)
    - **Competitive:** Compare against direct rivals.
    - **Functional:** Compare across industries (e.g., Xerox benchmarked logistics against L.L. Bean; Southwest benchmarked aircraft turnaround against NASCAR pit stops).
    - **Internal:** Compare across your own divisions or locations.
- **Common Implementation Pitfalls**
    
    - Ignoring the strategic context — optimizing a process that should not exist.
    - Designing without frontline input.
    - Expecting only radical results and ignoring incremental gains.
    - Overlooking the human impact — resistance kills initiatives regardless of analytical quality.
    - Failing to sustain — metrics improve for a quarter, then drift back.

## Quality & Performance

### **Why Quality Matters**

- **Definition & Strategic Role**
    - How is quality defined in operations? (The alignment between intent, execution, and human expectation — does the output meet what was promised?)
    - Why is quality embedded in every process? (It shows up in design, sourcing, operations, and service — not just at the inspection stage.)
    - What is the competitive case for quality? (Firms known for quality outperform rivals through trust, lower failure costs, and customer retention.)

### **Costs of Quality**

Five categories — understand what each covers and when each is incurred.

- **Prevention Costs** — investments to avoid defects _before_ they occur.
    
    - Examples: training, quality planning, supplier development, process redesign.
- **Appraisal Costs** — costs of measuring and monitoring to catch issues _early_.
    
    - Examples: inspection, testing, audits, supplier ratings.
- **Internal Failure Costs** — defects found _before_ the product reaches the customer.
    
    - Examples: rework, scrap, waste discovered during production.
- **External Failure Costs** — defects found _after_ the customer receives the product.
    
    - Examples: repairs, returns, complaints, warranty claims, litigation.
- **Ethical Failure Costs** — costs from deception or negligence that knowingly passes defects along.
    
    - Examples: cover-ups, negligence leading to fines, lawsuits, brand damage, and societal harm.
- ***Think about it***
    
    - Which cost category is most expensive in the long run, and why? (External and ethical failure — by the time the defect reaches the customer, the damage to trust and brand is far more costly than catching it earlier.)
    - What is the trade-off between prevention/appraisal and failure costs? (Investing more upfront in prevention and appraisal reduces the larger downstream failure costs.)

### **Total Quality Management (TQM)**

- **Three Pillars**
    
    - **Customer Satisfaction** — What does this mean in practice? (Meeting or exceeding expectations across design, delivery, value, support, and experience — including conformance to specs and fitness for use.)
        
    - **Employee Involvement** — What does this require? (Everyone owns quality. Workers serve internal and external customers and solve problems at the source. Cross-functional teamwork, not just a QC department.)
        
    - **Continuous Improvement (Kaizen)** — What is the underlying logic? (Ongoing, incremental effort to reduce waste, errors, and variation. Structured through cycles like Plan-Do-Check-Act.)
        
- **What TQM Requires to Succeed**
    
    - Management commitment and leadership to build a _culture_ of quality — not just tools or programs.

### **Six Sigma**

- **Core Goal**
    
    - What is Six Sigma's target? (No more than 3.4 defects per million opportunities — DPMO.)
    - What does Six Sigma add that TQM does not? (Statistical rigor and a structured project methodology — it extends TQM rather than replacing it.)
- **The DMAIC Cycle**

	| Step    | What It Does                                                         |
	| ------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------- |
	| Define  | Establish scope, boundaries, and customer-critical outputs           |
	| Measure | Collect performance data — quality, time, cost, errors, satisfaction |
	| Analyze | Identify gaps, bottlenecks, and root causes                          |
	| Improve | Redesign the process with justifiable changes                        |
	| Control | Monitor performance to sustain improvements                          |
    
- **TQM vs. Six Sigma — Key Distinctions**

	
	|Dimension|TQM|Six Sigma|
	|---|---|---|
	|Focus|Broad organizational culture; continuous improvement everywhere|Targeted reduction of variation and defects via projects|
	|Methods|Qualitative: empowerment, culture, customer focus|Quantitative: DMAIC, statistical analysis, process capability|
	|Metrics|Customer satisfaction scores, qualitative outcomes|DPMO — defects per million opportunities|
	|Who drives it|Everyone — quality as shared responsibility|Trained specialists: Black Belts, Master Black Belts|
	    


### **Statistical Process Control (SPC) — Core Concepts**

- **What is SPC?**
    
    - What problem does SPC solve? (Distinguishing between normal process variation and signals that something has actually gone wrong.)
    - **Common cause variation:** What is it? (Natural, random variation inherent to any process — expected and acceptable.)
    - **Assignable cause variation:** What is it? (Unusual variation with a specific, identifiable cause — signals the process is out of control and needs investigation.)
- **Control Charts — The Basic Logic**
    
    - What does a control chart do? (Plots process data over time and compares each point against statistically derived control limits to detect when a process has shifted.)
    - What are the three lines on any control chart? (UCL — Upper Control Limit; CL — Center Line; LCL — Lower Control Limit.)
    - What does it mean when a point falls outside the control limits? (The process is out of control — an assignable cause likely exists and should be investigated.)
    - **Key distinction:** Are control limits the same as specification limits? (No. Control limits are derived from the process's own variation. Specification limits are set by customer or design requirements. A process can be in control but still produce defects if it is not _capable_.)
    
### **Variables vs. Attributes Data**

Before selecting a control chart, you need to know what _type_ of data you are working with — this determines everything else.

- **Variables data** — measurements on a continuous scale. There is a meaningful number behind each observation: weight, length, time, temperature. You can be more or less of it.
    
    - Examples: bag weight in grams, processing time in seconds, hole diameter in mm.
- **Attributes data** — counts or classifications. Each unit is either defective or not, or you are counting how many defects appear. There is no "how much" — just yes/no or a tally.
    
    - Examples: a motherboard that passes or fails inspection, the number of complaints on a letter.
- **Why it matters for control charts:**
    
    |Data Type|What You're Tracking|Chart to Use|
    |---|---|---|
    |Variables|Continuous measurements in small groups|X̄ and R Chart|
    |Attributes — proportion defective|Fraction of items that fail (yes/no per item)|p-Chart|
    |Attributes — defect count|Number of defects on one unit|c-Chart|
    


### **Choosing the Right Control Chart**

Three chart types — the choice depends on _what_ you are measuring.

- **X̄ and R Chart** — _"How consistent is my process output?"_
    
    - **Use when:** Measuring something on a continuous scale (weight, time, length, temperature) and taking small groups of measurements at a time (n = 2–10).
    - **X̄ chart** tracks whether the process _average_ is drifting. **R chart** tracks whether the process _spread_ is widening or tightening. Always run them together.
    - What could go wrong if you only look at the X̄ chart? (A process can look fine on average but be wildly inconsistent underneath — the R chart catches this.)
- **p-Chart** — _"What fraction of my output is defective?"_
    
    - **Use when:** Inspecting a batch and asking a yes/no question about each item — defective or not. Sample sizes are large (n = 50+).
    - What does p̄ represent? (The overall average proportion defective across all samples.)
- **c-Chart** — _"How many defects are on each unit?"_
    
    - **Use when:** Counting how many individual defects appear on a single inspected unit — not whether the unit passed or failed overall.
    - What is the key difference from a p-chart? (A p-chart marks a unit "defective" or not. A c-chart counts _all defects on that unit_ — one unit can have multiple defects.)
- **The One-Sentence Rule**
    
	| Chart       | When to Use                                                       |
	| ----------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------- |
	| **X̄ & R**  | You're measuring something and taking it in groups                |
	| **p-chart** | You're counting defective items as a fraction of a batch          |
	| **c-chart** | You're counting defects on a single unit (one unit can have many) |
	    

### **Control Chart Formulas & Steps**

#### X̄ and R Chart

|Step|What to Calculate|Formula|
|---|---|---|
|1|Sample mean for each sample|$\bar{X} = \dfrac{x_1 + x_2 + \cdots + x_n}{n}$|
|2|Range for each sample|$R = x_{\max} - x_{\min}$|
|3|Grand mean and average range|$\bar{\bar{X}} = \dfrac{\sum \bar{X}}{k}, \quad \bar{R} = \dfrac{\sum R}{k}$|
|4|Look up constants for sample size $n$|$A_2, D_3, D_4$ from the control chart constants table|
|5|X̄ chart control limits|$UCL = \bar{\bar{X}} + A_2\bar{R}, \quad LCL = \bar{\bar{X}} - A_2\bar{R}$|
|6|R chart control limits|$UCL = D_4\bar{R}, \quad LCL = D_3\bar{R}$|

> **Note:** $n$ is the number of observations _within_ each sample, not the number of samples. Use $n$ to look up constants.

#### p-Chart

|Step|What to Calculate|Formula|
|---|---|---|
|1|Defect rate for each sample|$p_i = \dfrac{\text{defects}}{n}$|
|2|Overall proportion defective|$\bar{p} = \dfrac{\text{total defects}}{\text{total observations}}$|
|3|Standard deviation|$\sigma_p = \sqrt{\dfrac{\bar{p}(1-\bar{p})}{n}}$|
|4|Control limits|$UCL = \bar{p} + 3\sigma_p, \quad LCL = \bar{p} - 3\sigma_p$|

> If LCL is negative, set it to 0.

#### c-Chart

|Step|What to Calculate|Formula|
|---|---|---|
|1|Average defects per unit|$\bar{c} = \dfrac{\sum c}{k}$|
|2|Standard deviation|$\sigma = \sqrt{\bar{c}}$|
|3|Control limits|$UCL = \bar{c} + 3\sqrt{\bar{c}}, \quad LCL = \bar{c} - 3\sqrt{\bar{c}}$|

> Why is $\sigma = \sqrt{\bar{c}}$ for the c-chart? (Defect counts follow a Poisson distribution, where the standard deviation equals the square root of the mean — no sample size needed.)

### **Process Capability**

- **What Is Process Capability?**
    
    - What question does capability answer? (Is the process _centered well enough and tight enough_ to consistently meet the customer's specification?)
    - What is the difference between a process being _in control_ and being _capable_? (In control means variation is stable and predictable. Capable means that stable variation actually fits within the specification limits. A process can be in control but incapable.)
- **Cp — Process Capability Ratio** (measures spread only)
    

$$C_p = \frac{USL - LSL}{6\sigma}$$

- What does Cp tell you? (Whether the process spread is narrow enough to fit within the spec range — _ignoring_ whether the process is centered.)
    
- **Cpk — Process Capability Index** (accounts for centering)
    

$$C_{pk} = \min\left(\frac{USL - \mu}{3\sigma},\ \frac{\mu - LSL}{3\sigma}\right)$$

- What does Cpk tell you? (How the process is actually performing, accounting for where the mean sits relative to both spec limits. Always ≤ Cp.)
    
- What does it mean when Cpk < Cp? (The process is off-center — the mean is closer to one spec limit than the other.)
    
- **Interpreting Capability Values**
    
	|Cpk Value|What It Means|
	|---|---|
	|< 1.00|Not capable — defects are escaping the spec|
	|1.00 – 1.33|Barely capable — meets 3σ standard, needs monitoring|
	|1.33 – 1.67|Good — meets 4σ standard|
	|≥ 2.00|Excellent — meets 6σ standard|
    
- ***Think about it***
    
    - A process has Cp = 1.40 but Cpk = 0.85. What does this tell you? (The spread is potentially capable, but the process is significantly off-center — the mean is sitting near one of the spec limits. Fix centering first before attacking variability.)
    - Can a process have Cpk > Cp? (No — Cpk is always ≤ Cp. Cpk = Cp only when the process is perfectly centered.)

## Lean Systems

### **The Philosophy of Lean**

- **Core Definition**
    - What is Lean? (A philosophy of eliminating waste, maximizing customer value, pursuing continuous improvement, and respecting people — not just a set of tools.)
    - What is the relationship between quality and waste? (Every time quality slips, waste is created — defective work, rework, returns, and lost time are all forms of waste.)
    - What does Lean ultimately seek to create? (Flow — the smooth, uninterrupted movement of value toward the customer, with no waiting, no excess, and no defects.)

### **The 8 Wastes (Muda)**

Every waste either consumes resources without adding value or hides a problem that needs to be solved. Know each type and be able to identify it in a scenario.

|Waste|What It Is|Example|
|---|---|---|
|**Overproduction**|Making more than needed, or before it's needed|Producing 500 units when demand is 300 — inventory piles up and hides defects|
|**Waiting**|Idle time between process steps|Workers or machines sitting idle waiting for materials, approvals, or the prior step|
|**Transportation**|Unnecessary movement of materials or products|Routing parts through three warehouses when one would do|
|**Motion**|Unnecessary movement by people|A worker walking across the shop repeatedly because tools aren't co-located|
|**Inventory**|Excess raw materials, WIP, or finished goods|Stocking 10 days of components when 2 days would suffice|
|**Inappropriate Processing**|Using more complex or expensive methods than needed|Running a part through a CNC machine when a simple drill press would work|
|**Defects**|Errors, rework, or corrections|Mislabeled products, returned shipments, re-inspection|
|**Underutilization of People**|Failing to use employees' skills, ideas, or creativity|Workers who see process improvements but aren't empowered to act|

- **The Water-and-Rocks Metaphor**
    - What does it illustrate? (Inventory is the water; problems — defects, downtime, poor quality — are rocks on the riverbed. High inventory hides problems. Lowering inventory exposes them, forcing you to solve them rather than buffer around them.)
    - Why is this insight important? (It reframes inventory not as a safety net but as a symptom — a sign that something upstream is unreliable.)

### **Push vs. Pull**

- **Push System**
    
    - How does it work? (Work is released into the process based on a schedule — each step produces and pushes output to the next, regardless of whether the next step is ready.)
    - What is the problem? (WIP accumulates between steps, lead times grow, and defects are buried in large batches before anyone notices.)
- **Pull System**
    
    - How does it work? (Production is triggered only by actual downstream demand — nothing is made until the next step or customer requests it.)
    - What does this accomplish? (Limits WIP, synchronizes the process to real demand, and surfaces problems quickly.)
    - What is the mechanism? (Kanban signals — cards, empty containers, or electronic flags — authorize upstream production only when downstream capacity exists.)

### **Value Stream Mapping (VSM)**

- **What It Is**
    
    - What is a Value Stream Map? (A visual tool that documents the entire flow of material and information from raw material to customer, in the current state, to expose waste and design a leaner future state.)
    - What does the map show? (Process steps, inventory buffers between steps, cycle times, setup times, uptime, WIP levels, and the information flow that triggers production.)
- **The Five-Step VSM Process**
    
    1. Select a product or service family.
    2. Map the **current state** — document how work actually flows today.
    3. Identify waste, delays, and non-value-added time.
    4. Design the **future state** — the ideal lean flow.
    5. Develop an implementation plan.
- **The VSM Timeline — The Key Insight**
    
    - What do the high peaks on the timeline represent? (Waiting time — WIP sitting in queues between steps. Non-value-added.)
    - What do the low valleys represent? (Actual processing time at each step. Value-added.)
    - Why is this contrast important? (In most processes, 95–99% of total lead time is waiting. The visual gap between peaks and valleys shows exactly where to focus improvement — and it is almost never on making individual steps faster.)
- **Five Key Measures from a VSM**
    
    |Measure|What It Tells You|How to Calculate|
    |---|---|---|
    |**Takt Time**|How fast must we produce to match customer demand?|Available production time ÷ daily demand|
    |**Bottleneck**|Which step limits the whole system's output?|Step with the longest cycle time (check effective C/T = C/T ÷ uptime)|
    |**Total Processing Time**|How much actual work touches the product?|Sum of all cycle times|
    |**Production Lead Time**|How long does a unit spend in the system?|Sum of (WIP ÷ daily demand) for each inventory buffer|
    |**Process Cycle Efficiency**|What % of lead time is value-added?|Total processing time ÷ production lead time × 100|
    
- ***Think about it***
    
    - If a step's cycle time exceeds takt time, what does that mean? (That step cannot keep up with customer demand — it is a capacity problem that must be addressed.)
    - A process has a total processing time of 45 seconds and a production lead time of 6 days. What does a PCE of ~0.01% tell you? (Nearly all time in the system is waste — the opportunity for improvement lies in attacking waiting time and WIP, not in speeding up individual process steps.)

### **Kanban Systems**

- **What Kanban Is**
    
    - What is a Kanban system? (A visual signal system — card, empty container, or screen — that authorizes upstream production or replenishment only when downstream demand has actually occurred.)
    - What problem does it solve? (It makes pull systems operational — the signal is the mechanism that prevents overproduction and limits WIP.)
- **How It Works**
    
    - Each container of parts has a Kanban card or signal.
    - When the downstream station empties a container, the empty container (or card) travels back upstream as an authorization to produce more.
    - The upstream process makes _only_ what was requested — nothing more.
- **Two Types**
    
    - **Single-card system:** One card controls material movement — simple replenishment pull.
    - **Two-card system:** One card for withdrawal (move parts), one card for production (make parts) — used when production and conveyance are separated.
- **The Kanban Formula**
    

$$k = \frac{d(v + r)(1 + a)}{c}$$

|Variable|Meaning|
|---|---|
|$k$|Number of containers to authorize|
|$d$|Daily demand (units/day)|
|$v$|Waiting/handling time per cycle (days) — non-value-added|
|$r$|Processing time per container (days) — value-added|
|$a$|Safety factor (e.g., 0.10 = 10%)|
|$c$|Units per container|

- **The Logic Behind the Formula**
    
    - What does $(v + r)$ represent? (The total replenishment lead time — the window during which you are waiting for a full container to return.)
    - What does $d \times (v + r)$ represent? (The demand that accumulates during that window — the minimum inventory you need to avoid a stockout.)
    - Why divide by $c$? (To convert units needed into containers, since Kanban circulates containers, not individual units.)
    - Always round $k$ **up** — you cannot have a fraction of a container, and rounding down guarantees a stockout.
- **Lean Connection**
    
    - How does reducing $v$ affect $k$? (Directly reduces the number of containers needed — attacking non-value-added waiting time shrinks authorized WIP. This is Lean in action: eliminating waste has a measurable, quantitative impact.)

### **The Toyota Production System (TPS) — House of Toyota**

- **The Goal (Roof)**
    
    - Highest quality, lowest cost, shortest lead time — achieved through the continuous elimination of waste.
- **Two Pillars**
    
    - **Just-in-Time (JIT):** Produce only what is needed, when it is needed, in the exact amount needed. Enabled by takt time, one-piece flow, and pull systems.
    - **Jidoka (Autonomation):** Build quality into the process. Workers and machines are empowered — and expected — to stop the line when something goes wrong. Includes:
        - **Poka-Yoke** (error-proofing): Design the process so mistakes are hard or impossible to make.
        - **Andon:** Visual signals (lights, boards) that make problems immediately visible.
- **The Foundation**
    
    - **Heijunka:** Leveling production volume and mix over time to smooth demand across workstations and reduce stress on the system.
    - **Standard Work:** Documented best-known method for each task — the baseline from which improvement is measured.
    - **5S:** Sort, Straighten, Shine, Standardize, Sustain — a workplace organization system that creates visual order and makes abnormalities immediately apparent.
    - **Total Productive Maintenance (TPM):** Operators take ownership of basic equipment maintenance to improve reliability and uptime.
- **The Core**
    
    - Continuous improvement (Kaizen) + respect for people. TPS fails without both.

### **Examples of lean principles in  action - Lean Cell Designs**

- **One-Worker, Multiple-Machine (OWMM) Cell**
    
    - What is it? (One cross-trained worker operates several machines in sequence, typically arranged in a U-shape or circle.)
    - What does it eliminate? (Idle machine time, excess WIP between machines, and wasted motion.)
- **Group Technology (GT) Cells**
    
    - What is it? (Parts with similar processing requirements are grouped into a "family" and processed together through a dedicated cell of machines.)
    - What does it simplify? (Routing complexity, setup time, and material movement — the cell handles a family of parts rather than one-off routing through a job shop.)

### **Implementation Challenges**

- What are the most common reasons Lean initiatives fail?
    - Building tools before culture — Lean requires trust, empowerment, and continuous improvement mindset; tools alone accomplish nothing.
    - Failing to involve frontline employees in Kaizen.
    - Supplier unreliability undermining the pull system.
    - Inflexible reward systems or labor structures that resist cross-training.
    - Post-COVID lesson: pure efficiency without resilience leaves systems fragile — some buffering capacity may be strategically justified.

## Managing Constraints

### **The Philosophy of TOC**

- **Where TOC Fits Among the Three Frameworks**


	| Framework | Core Philosophy               | One-Line Idea                |
	| --------- | ----------------------------- | ---------------------------- |
	| **TQM**   | Perfection through discipline | "Do everything right."       |
	| **Lean**  | Perfection through flow       | "Do everything efficiently." |
	| **TOC**   | Perfection through focus      | "Do the right thing first."  |


- What is the fundamental TOC insight? (Improving everything equally doesn't improve the system — only improvements at the constraint move the needle. Focus beats dispersion.)
- What does "local efficiency ≠ global effectiveness" mean? (A step running at 100% utilization can actually hurt the system if it is not the bottleneck — it just creates WIP. Global performance only improves when the constraint improves.)

### **Key Definitions**

- **Capacity** — the maximum rate of output of a process or system.
- **Utilization** — the degree to which that capacity is actually being used.
- **Constraint** — any factor that limits the performance of a system and restricts its output. Can exist inside the firm (a machine, a policy, a workforce skill) or outside it (a supplier, a customer's order rate).
- **Bottleneck** — specifically, any resource whose available capacity limits the organization's ability to meet required volume or mix. Every system has at least one — otherwise output would be limited only by market demand.
    - What is the difference between a constraint and a bottleneck? (All bottlenecks are constraints, but not all constraints are bottlenecks. A bottleneck is a capacity constraint on a specific resource; a constraint is any factor limiting system performance.)

### **The Five Focusing Steps**

The core TOC improvement cycle — apply in order, then repeat.

| Step               | What to Do                                                                                                                | The Key Question                                    |
| ------------------ | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------- |
| **1. Identify**    | Find the step or resource most limiting system throughput                                                                 | "Where is the weakest link?"                        |
| **2. Exploit**     | Get the most out of the existing constraint — eliminate waste and downtime _at that point_ before spending any money      | "How do we squeeze every unit of output from it?"   |
| **3. Subordinate** | Align _everything else_ to support the constraint — no upstream step should produce faster than the bottleneck can absorb | "How do we synchronize the whole system around it?" |
| **4. Elevate**     | Once exploitation is maxed out, increase capacity through investment, redesign, or additional resources                   | "What breaks the limit permanently?"                |
| **5. Repeat**      | Once the constraint is broken, a new one will emerge — do not let inertia set in                                          | "What is the next constraint?"                      |


- ***Think about it ***
    - Why is Step 3 (Subordinate) so counter-intuitive? (It requires upstream stations to deliberately _slow down_ or sit idle — which looks inefficient locally but protects the bottleneck from being starved or flooded.)
    - Why must you return to Step 1 after elevating? (Fixing one constraint shifts the weakest link elsewhere. Continuous improvement requires continuously re-identifying the new constraint.)

### **TOC Operational Measures**

TOC connects three operational measures directly to financial outcomes — every improvement should be evaluated through this lens.

|Measure|TOC Definition|Financial Impact|
|---|---|---|
|**Throughput (T)**|Rate at which the system generates money through sales|↑ Throughput → ↑ Profit, ↑ ROI, ↑ Cash Flow|
|**Inventory (I)**|All money invested in things the system intends to sell|↓ Inventory → ↑ Profit, ↑ ROI, ↑ Cash Flow|
|**Operating Expense (OE)**|All money spent turning inventory into throughput|↓ OE → ↑ Profit, ↑ ROI, ↑ Cash Flow|

- What does the TOC financial lens change about how we evaluate decisions? (A decision that improves local efficiency but doesn't increase throughput at the constraint is not an improvement — it just moves cost around.)

### **TOC Product Mix Decisions**

When a bottleneck limits total output, you cannot make everything at maximum demand. The question becomes: _which products should get the scarce bottleneck time?_

- **Traditional Method (rank by contribution margin)**
    
    - How does it work? (Produce as much of the highest-CM product as possible, then the next, until capacity runs out.)
    - What is its flaw? (It ignores _how much bottleneck time_ each product consumes. A high-CM product that hogs the bottleneck may be less profitable than a lower-CM product that uses the constraint efficiently.)
- **Bottleneck Method (rank by CM per bottleneck minute)**
    
    - How does it work? (Calculate contribution margin per unit of bottleneck time for each product, rank highest to lowest, and allocate bottleneck capacity in that order.)
    - Why does this outperform the traditional method? (It maximizes the revenue generated per scarce resource — the bottleneck — rather than per unit.)
    - Special case: if a product **does not use the bottleneck at all**, it is automatically first in the queue — it is "free" from the bottleneck's perspective.
- **The Two Key Formulas**
    

$$\text{Contribution Margin} = \text{Price} - \text{Raw Material Cost}$$

$$\text{CM per Bottleneck Minute} = \frac{\text{Contribution Margin}}{\text{Time at Bottleneck (min/unit)}}$$

- ***Think about it***
    - Why is labor excluded from contribution margin in TOC? (In TOC, labor is treated as a fixed cost — workers are paid regardless of utilization. Only truly variable costs (raw materials) are subtracted.)
    - Why can the bottleneck method produce higher profit with _fewer_ units of one product? (By reallocating bottleneck time from a bottleneck-heavy, lower-efficiency product to a bottleneck-light, higher-efficiency one, total throughput value increases even if one product's quantity drops.)

### **Drum-Buffer-Rope (DBR)**

DBR is the scheduling system TOC uses to operationalize the Five Focusing Steps in a production environment.

- **Drum** — the bottleneck sets the production pace for the entire system. Everything else follows its beat.
    
- **Buffer** — a time or inventory buffer placed _before_ the bottleneck (and before shipping) to protect it from disruption. If the bottleneck ever goes idle due to upstream problems, throughput is permanently lost.
    
- **Rope** — a release signal tied back to the start of the line. Raw materials are only released at the rate the bottleneck can absorb them — preventing WIP from piling up upstream.
    
- **What DBR prevents:**
    
    - Overproduction upstream (the rope holds back early release).
    - Bottleneck starvation (the buffer absorbs variability before it reaches the constraint).
    - Excess WIP accumulation throughout the system.
- ***Think about it***
    
    - How does DBR relate to a pull system? (DBR is not a pure pull system — the rope is tied to the bottleneck's pace, not to downstream demand directly. But it shares the pull logic of not releasing more than the system can absorb.)

### **Line Balancing**

- **What It Is**
    
    - What problem does line balancing solve? (How to distribute a fixed set of tasks across workstations so that no station is overloaded, idle time is minimized, and the line produces at the desired rate.)
    - Where is it applied? (Line processes — high-volume, standardized production where work moves sequentially through fixed stations: appliance assembly, auto manufacturing, fast food.)
- **Five Key Terms**
    
|Term|Definition|Formula|
|---|---|---|
|**Desired Output Rate (r)**|How many units the line must produce per time period|$r = \dfrac{\text{Demand}}{\text{Available time}}$|
|**Cycle Time (c)**|Maximum time allowed at each workstation|$c = \dfrac{\text{Available time}}{\text{Demand}}$|
|**Theoretical Minimum (TM)**|Fewest stations possible if work divided perfectly|$TM = \dfrac{\text{Total task time}}{c}$, round up|
|**Efficiency**|Fraction of total capacity used for actual work|$\dfrac{\text{Total task time}}{\text{Stations} \times c} \times 100%$|
|**Balance Delay**|Fraction of total capacity that is idle|$1 - \text{Efficiency}$|
    
 **Important:** Cycle time in line balancing is _not_ the same as cycle time in VSM. In VSM, cycle time = how fast a step actually works. In line balancing, cycle time = the _maximum allowed_ time per station, set by demand. Same term, different meaning.
    
- **Precedence Constraints**
    
    - What are they? (Some tasks must be completed before others can begin — you cannot attach the agitator before inserting the shaft.)
    - What is a precedence diagram? (A network diagram — nodes are tasks, arrows show which tasks must precede others. It makes the available sequencing options visible.)
- **The Assignment Algorithm**
    
    - For each station, repeat until no more tasks fit:
        1. List **candidates** — tasks whose predecessors are all already assigned.
        2. Check **fit** — only candidates whose task time ≤ remaining station time.
        3. Apply the **assignment rule** to pick among candidates.
        4. Assign the task; subtract its time from the station's remaining capacity.
    - When no candidates fit, close the station and open a new one.
- **Assignment Rules** (heuristics — no single rule guarantees the optimal solution)
    
|Rule|Logic|
|---|---|
|**Longest task time**|Assign the biggest task first — hardest to fit later|
|**Most followers**|Assign the task with the most downstream dependents — keeps future options open|
|**Shortest task time**|Opposite of longest — fills gaps with small tasks|
|**Fewest followers**|Opposite of most followers|
    
- **Managerial Considerations**
    
    - **Behavioral:** Highly paced, repetitive work reduces job satisfaction and increases turnover. Task variety or small buffers can help.
    - **Pacing:** Products moving automatically at each cycle time improves efficiency but reduces flexibility during disruptions.
    - **Mixed-model lines:** Multiple product variants increase flexibility but complicate scheduling and balancing.
- **Line Balancing vs. TOC — Same Problem, Different Lens**
    
	|Dimension|Line Balancing|Theory of Constraints|
	|---|---|---|
	|Focus|Distribute work evenly across all stations|Identify and exploit the single binding constraint|
	|Goal|Minimize idle time; maximize efficiency|Maximize system throughput|
	|When to use|Designing or re-designing a line for new demand|Improving an existing line with a known constraint|

## Capacity Management

### **What Is Capacity?**

- **Core Definitions**
    - What is capacity? (The maximum rate of output of a process or system — the most that can flow through the pipe per unit of time.)
    - What is utilization? (The degree to which available capacity is actually being used.)
    - What is a capacity cushion? (The reserve capacity a process keeps to handle sudden demand increases or temporary production losses — measured as the percentage by which average utilization falls below 100%.)
    - Why maintain a cushion rather than running at 100% utilization? (Processes running at full utilization have no buffer against variability — any disruption immediately causes missed demand or delays.)

### **Economies and Diseconomies of Scale**

- **Economies of Scale**
    
    - What causes them? (Fixed costs spread over more units, bulk purchasing lowers input costs, and specialization improves efficiency — average cost per unit falls as output rises.)
- **Diseconomies of Scale**
    
    - What causes them? (Beyond an optimal size, coordination complexity, bureaucracy, and loss of operational focus drive average cost back up.)
- **The Sweet Spot**
    
    - What is the managerial implication? (Every process has an optimal scale where average cost is minimized. Too small = underutilized fixed costs. Too large = coordination overhead. The goal is to find and operate near that point.)
    - Example: A 250-bed hospital has high cost per patient (underused); a 500-bed hospital hits optimal scale; a 750-bed hospital faces diseconomies from coordination overhead.

### **Capacity Timing Strategies**

When demand is growing, _when_ you expand matters as much as _how much_ you expand.

- **Expansionist Strategy**
    
    - What is it? (Large, infrequent capacity jumps — stay ahead of demand.)
    - What are the benefits? (Economies of scale, faster learning, competitive deterrence — can support a cost leadership position.)
    - What is the risk? (Excess capacity during periods when demand hasn't yet materialized.)
- **Wait-and-See Strategy**
    
    - What is it? (Smaller, more frequent increments — follow demand rather than lead it.)
    - What are the benefits? (Lower risk of overcapacity; demand is more certain before each investment.)
    - What is the trade-off? (May miss economies of scale; risk of falling behind demand if growth accelerates.)
- **Follow the Leader**
    
    - An intermediate approach where firms match competitors' capacity moves rather than leading or lagging independently.
- **Key Relationship**
    
    - Why are timing and sizing linked? (If demand is growing and you lengthen the time between expansions, each increment must be larger to bridge the gap. The two decisions cannot be made independently.)

### **Systematic Approach to Long-Term Capacity Decisions**

A four-step framework for structured capacity planning.

- **Step 1 — Estimate Capacity Requirements**
    - What is the goal? (Determine how much capacity the process needs over a future planning horizon to satisfy demand while maintaining the desired cushion.)
    - **Output measures:** Used when the product mix is stable and output is homogeneous (e.g., units per week, patients per day).
    - **Input measures:** Used when product variety is high, mix shifts, or learning effects are significant. Capacity is expressed in input units — machines, workers, trucks.
    - What is the capacity requirement formula using input measures?

$$M = \frac{\sum_p \left[\frac{D_p \times p_p}{N} + \frac{D_p}{Q_p} \times s_p\right]}{1 - \frac{c}{100}}$$


Where: $D_p$ = annual demand for product $p$, $p_p$ = processing time per unit, $N$ = total available time per machine/worker per year, $Q_p$ = lot size, $s_p$ = setup time per lot, $c$ = desired capacity cushion (%).


- **Step 2 — Identify Gaps**
    
    - What is a capacity gap? (The difference between projected capacity requirement and current capacity — positive means shortage, negative means excess.)
    - What complicates this step? (Multiple products sharing the same resource; expanding one stage only helps if it is the bottleneck — expanding non-bottleneck capacity adds cost without adding throughput.)
- **Step 3 — Develop Alternatives**
    
    - What is the base case? (Do nothing — accept lost sales or idle costs.)
    - Short-term options to increase capacity: overtime, extra shifts, temporary labor, subcontracting.
    - Long-term options: new facilities, facility expansion, expansionist vs. wait-and-see timing strategy.
    - Options to reduce capacity: close sites, scale down operations.
- **Step 4 — Evaluate Alternatives**
    
    - **Qualitative factors:** Strategic fit, flexibility, demand uncertainty, competitive dynamics, technology risk.
    - **Quantitative factors:** Estimate cash flows for each option vs. the base case; use NPV or scenario analysis to assess financial impact.
    - What is the correct decision process? (Combine quantitative analysis with managerial judgment — numbers alone do not capture strategic risk or competitive context.)
