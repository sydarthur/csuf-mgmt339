## PART 1 — PROCESS STRATEGY

---

### 1.1 What Is a Process?

A process is a sequence of activities that transforms inputs (labor, materials, information, equipment) into outputs (goods or services) that deliver value to a customer. Every product or service that exists is the result of a process. Faulty processes destroy competitive advantage — no amount of good intention fixes a broken process.

Process decisions are **strategic** — they must align with the firm's long-term competitive goals across four dimensions:

|Dimension|What It Controls|
|---|---|
|**Quality**|Does the output meet the standard?|
|**Flexibility**|Can we handle different requests?|
|**Time**|How fast do we deliver?|
|**Cost**|Can we produce efficiently?|

---

### 1.2 Process Divergence & Process Flow

Two fundamental characteristics define how a process is structured:

**Process Divergence** — the degree to which tasks are customized, with latitude in how they are performed.

- **High divergence:** flexible, unique paths for each customer or product
- **Low divergence:** standardized tasks, same sequence every time

**Process Flow** — how work is organized and sequenced.

- **Flexible flow:** tasks can occur in many different orders
- **Line flow:** tasks follow a fixed, repeatable sequence

> **The rule:** More customization → higher divergence → more flexible flow. More standardization → lower divergence → tighter line flow. These two always move together.

---

### 1.3 The Customer-Contact Matrix (Services)

Used to classify service processes based on how much the customer interacts with the process and how customized the service is.

|Type|Customer Contact|Customization|Flow|Examples|
|---|---|---|---|---|
|**Front Office**|High|High|Flexible|Consulting, law, therapy|
|**Hybrid Office**|Medium|Medium|Flexible with dominant paths|Hospital, bank branch|
|**Back Office**|Low|Low|Line|Check processing, online transactions|

---

### 1.4 The Product-Process Matrix (Manufacturing)

Used to classify manufacturing processes based on volume and customization.

|Type|Volume|Customization|Flow|Examples|
|---|---|---|---|---|
|**Job Process**|Low|Very high|Flexible|Custom furniture, bespoke tailoring|
|**Batch Process**|Low–Moderate|Moderate|Disconnected line|Bakeries, craft breweries, clothing|
|**Line Process**|High|Low|Connected line|Automobile assembly, fast food|
|**Continuous Flow**|Very high|None|Continuous|Oil refining, paper mills, chemicals|

---

### 1.5 The Combined Matrix

Manufacturing and service process types map onto the same spectrum:

```
High Customization ◄─────────────────────────────► Low Customization

Job Process         Batch Process      Line Process      Continuous Flow
Front Office        Hybrid Office      Back Office
```

**Strategic fit:** A firm must sit on the correct diagonal. High customization with line-process infrastructure, or high standardization with job-process economics, both produce misalignment that shows up as either excessive cost or disappointed customers.

---

### 1.6 The Three Strategic Levers

Three decisions define how a process is configured. They always move together as you slide across the matrix.

|Lever|High Customization End|Low Customization End|
|---|---|---|
|**Customer Involvement**|High — customer shapes the output|Low — customer just receives it|
|**Resource Flexibility**|High — generalist workers & equipment|Low — specialized roles & machines|
|**Capital Intensity**|Low — labor-driven|High — automation-driven|

**Customer Involvement** — how much the customer participates in the process.

- High involvement examples: IKEA (self-assembly), ATMs, self-checkout
- Advantage: can lower cost, increase speed, improve personalization
- Disadvantage: introduces variability, requires interpersonal skills

**Resource Flexibility** — how much workers and equipment can handle varied tasks.

- Flexible workforce: cross-trained workers absorb fluctuations and bottlenecks
- Flexible equipment: general-purpose tools handle many product types
- Trade-off: flexibility costs efficiency — the more you can do, the less optimized you are at any one thing

**Capital Intensity** — the mix of automation vs. labor.

- **Fixed automation:** efficient at high volume, one product (assembly lines, refineries)
- **Flexible automation:** programmable, adaptable (robots, CNC machines, 3D printing)
- High investment requires high volume to justify — you cannot run a $10M automated line for 500 units per year

---

### 1.7 Production & Inventory Strategies

As customization decreases and standardization increases, firms shift how they manage production and inventory.

| Strategy                    | Finished Goods               | Engineering           | Lead Time | Examples                                    |
| --------------------------- | ---------------------------- | --------------------- | --------- | ------------------------------------------- |
| **DTO** (Design-to-Order)   | None                         | Full custom design    | Longest   | Bespoke architecture, custom shoes          |
| **MTO** (Make-to-Order)     | None                         | Minor adaptation      | Long      | Specialized medical equipment, custom homes |
| **ATO** (Assemble-to-Order) | None — subassemblies stocked | Configuration only    | Short     | Dell computers, car option packages         |
| **MTS** (Make-to-Stock)     | Finished goods ready         | Complete — no changes | Immediate | Packaged food, consumer electronics         |

> **The one-line story:** The further up, the more you are making it for one person from scratch. The further down, the more it was already made before they asked for it.

---

### 1.8 Manufacturing vs. Services — The Blurred Line

Traditionally: manufacturing makes tangible goods; services deliver intangible experiences. In practice, the most competitive companies blend both deliberately.

**Servitization** — when manufacturers wrap services around products to create recurring revenue and switching costs.

- **Apple:** manufactures hardware, monetizes through services (App Store, iCloud, Apple Music)
- **Tesla:** manufactures cars, delivers ongoing software updates and subscriptions
- **Rolls-Royce (engines):** sells thrust per flight hour, not the engine itself — "Power by the Hour"
- **John Deere:** sells precision agriculture data subscriptions alongside tractors

**Service companies adopting manufacturing discipline:**

- **Starbucks:** standardized recipes, centralized roasting, automated espresso machines — manufacturing logic applied to service at scale

## PART 2 — PROCESS ANALYSIS TOOLS

---

### 2.1 Productivity

Productivity is the most basic measure of process performance. It answers one question: **how much output are we getting per unit of input?**

$$\text{Productivity} = \frac{\text{Output}}{\text{Input}}$$

The input can be anything — labor, machines, time, materials. The choice of input determines which type of productivity you are measuring.

| Type                         | Formula                                  | When to Use                        |
| ---------------------------- | ---------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------- |
| **Labor Productivity**       | Output ÷ Labor hours                     | Single input, workforce efficiency |
| **Machine Productivity**     | Output ÷ Machine hours                   | Equipment utilization              |
| **Multifactor Productivity** | Output value (\$) ÷ Total input cost ($) | Multiple input types combined      |

**Why multifactor?** When labor, materials, and overhead are all involved, you cannot add hours to pounds to dollars. Convert everything to a common unit — dollar value — to get a single comparable ratio.

**Overhead** = everything it costs to keep the operation running that does not go directly into the product: rent, utilities, equipment depreciation, insurance, administrative salaries. Excluding it would make productivity look better than it really is.

#### Worked Example — Labor Productivity

> 4 workers produce 800 gift baskets in one week. Each works 6 hours/day, 5 days/week. What is hourly labor productivity?

Total labor hours = 4 × 6 × 5 = **120 hours**

$$\text{Productivity} = \frac{800}{120} = \textbf{6.67 baskets/hour}$$

#### Worked Example — Multifactor Productivity

> A bakery produces 500 loaves sold at $5 each. Costs: Labor $600, Materials $1,200, Overhead $400.

$$\text{MFP} = \frac{500 \times $5}{$600 + $1{,}200 + $400} = \frac{$2{,}500}{$2{,}200} = \textbf{1.14}$$

Above 1.0 means value is being created. Below 1.0 means inputs cost more than output is worth.

### 2.2 Learning Curve

As workers gain experience, the time per unit decreases. Every time cumulative output **doubles**, the time per unit falls by a constant percentage — the **learning rate**.

$$T_n = T_1 \cdot n^{\dfrac{\ln(r)}{\ln(2)}}$$

Where:

- $T_n$ = time to produce the $n$-th unit
- $T_1$ = time to produce the first unit
- $n$ = unit number you want to find
- $r$ = learning rate (e.g., 0.80 for 80%)

#### Worked Example

> First unit takes 100 hours. Learning rate = 80%. How long does the 4th unit take?

**Intuitive doubling check:**

- Unit 1: 100 hrs
- Unit 2: 100 × 0.80 = 80 hrs
- Unit 4: 80 × 0.80 = **64 hrs**

**Using the formula:**

$$T_4 = 100 \cdot 4^{\frac{\ln(0.80)}{\ln(2)}} = 100 \cdot 4^{-0.3219} = 100 \times 0.64 = \textbf{64 hours}$$

> **Why it matters:** If you bid on a contract or plan labor costs for a new product, you must account for the fact that early units are slow and expensive. Ignore the learning curve and you will underprice early production and run out of cash.

### 2.3 Flowcharts

A flowchart makes a process visible by mapping every step, decision point, and path. Before you can fix a process, you must be able to see it clearly.

**Three variants of flowcharts:**

**Simple Flowchart** — linear process map, one path or branching. Good for communicating a single process clearly.

**Swim Lane Flowchart** — assigns each department or role its own horizontal lane. Highlights handoffs between departments, which is where most process failures, delays, and miscommunications occur. Makes cross-functional dependencies visible.

**Service Blueprint** — specialized flowchart for services. Adds a **line of visibility** dividing:

- **Front stage** (above the line): what the customer sees and experiences
- **Back stage** (below the line): what the customer never sees but what makes the front stage possible

> Disney theme parks are a classic service blueprint in action — guests see the magic above the line; underground tunnels, logistics, and crowd flow management operate invisibly below it.

---

### 2.4 Process Charts

A process chart documents every single activity in a process and classifies each step into one of five categories. It requires time estimates and optionally distance data.

|Symbol|Type|Value-Adding?|Description|
|---|---|---|---|
|●|**Operation**|✅ Yes|Something is created, changed, or transformed|
|➔|**Transport**|❌ No|Subject physically moves from one place to another|
|■|**Inspect**|⚠️ Partly|Something is checked or verified without changing it|
|▶|**Delay**|❌ No|Waiting — nothing productive is happening|
|▼|**Storage**|❌ No|Items or people put away or held for later|

**Key insight:** In most processes, only a small fraction of total time is spent in Operations. The rest — transport, delays, inspection, storage — is waste that can be reduced or eliminated. A process chart makes this ratio visible and actionable.

**ER Example Summary (from textbook):**

|Activity|Steps|Time (min)|
|---|---|---|
|Operation|5|23|
|Transport|9|11|
|Inspect|2|8|
|Delay|3|8|
|**Total**|**19**|**50**|

Value-adding % = 23 ÷ 50 = **46%** — meaning more than half of the patient's time adds no value.

### 2.5 Data Analysis Tools

Once process data is collected, four tools help make sense of it and identify where to act.

**Histogram** Shows the frequency distribution of data. Reveals patterns — averages, variation, and outliers — that are invisible in a simple average. Use it when you want to understand the spread of a variable (e.g., order fulfillment times, service durations).

**Scatter Diagram** Plots two variables to reveal whether a relationship exists between them. Correlation does not prove causation, but it tells you where to investigate. Example: plot server response time vs. user retention to find the performance threshold that drives drop-off.

**Cause & Effect Diagram (Fishbone / Ishikawa)** Used when you know there is a problem but do not know why. The problem sits at the "head" of the fish. Causes are organized across branches, for e.g., Methods, Machines, Materials, Measurement, People, Environment. Forces structured thinking across all possible causes rather than jumping to the most obvious one.

**Pareto Chart** Based on the **80/20 rule** — roughly 80% of problems come from 20% of causes. A bar chart ranking causes from most to least frequent, overlaid with a cumulative % line. Tells you which few causes to fix first for maximum impact. Essential when resources are limited and you cannot fix everything at once.

### 2.6 Redesigning Processes

Two approaches to improving a process:

**Process Reengineering** — radical redesign, starting from scratch. High risk, high reward. Ask: "If we were building this today with no legacy constraints, what would it look like?" Example: Amazon did not improve physical retail — they rebuilt the model entirely.

**Process Improvement** — incremental, ongoing changes. Lower risk, compounds over time. Toyota's _kaizen_ philosophy: every worker suggests small improvements continuously. Thousands of tiny improvements over years create enormous competitive advantage.

Both require **Process Analysis** first — you cannot know what to reengineer or improve until you have measured what is actually happening.

**Questioning & Brainstorming** — for every step, ask: What? When? Who? Where? How? How well? And most importantly — **Why?** If the answer to "why do we do this?" is "because we always have," that step is on trial.

**Benchmarking** — comparing your process against a best-in-class operation:

- **Competitive:** compare against direct rivals
- **Functional:** compare across industries (Xerox benchmarked logistics against L.L. Bean; Southwest benchmarked aircraft turnaround against NASCAR pit crews)
- **Internal:** compare across your own divisions or locations

**Common implementation pitfalls:**

- Ignoring the strategic context — optimizing a process that should not exist
- Designing without frontline input — the people doing the work know things no report captures
- Expecting only radical results — incremental gains compound dramatically
- Ignoring the human impact — process change affects people's jobs and routines; resistance kills initiatives regardless of analytical quality
- Failing to sustain — metrics improve for a quarter, then everyone moves on and processes drift back

## PART 3 — ANSWER KEY

---

**A1.** The urgent care clinic sits in the **Hybrid Office** — there is meaningful patient interaction (intake, consultation) but the process is largely standardized with defined protocols. The analogous inventory strategy is **MTO or ATO** — the "product" (diagnosis and treatment) is somewhat standardized but still assembled around the patient's specific presenting condition.

---

**A2.**

|Craft Distillery|Large Spirits Company|
|---|---|---|
|Process Type|Batch|Continuous Flow|
|Customer Involvement|Low (B2B, niche)|Very Low|
|Resource Flexibility|High — workers handle multiple tasks|Low — specialized roles|
|Capital Intensity|Low — labor and general equipment|Very High — automated lines|

The distillery cannot justify expensive dedicated automation for low-volume seasonal batches. The large producer runs 24/7 automation because volume makes it the only economically rational choice.

---

**A3.** Chipotle is best classified as a **Hybrid Office**. There is meaningful customer interaction at each station — customers make choices at every step, which introduces flexibility and some divergence. However, the overall flow is standardized: every customer moves through the same assembly line sequence in the same order. It is not a Back Office (too much involvement) and not a Front Office (not enough divergence — no truly unique process per customer). This is a deliberate strategic choice: Chipotle achieves perceived customization within a tightly controlled line flow.

---

**A4.** Moving from job to batch process:

- **Customer Involvement** decreases — less one-on-one customization per order
- **Resource Flexibility** decreases — workers become more specialized, equipment more dedicated
- **Capital Intensity** increases — investment in batch production equipment

Risks: losing customers who valued full customization, worker resistance to narrower roles, over-investment in equipment before volume justifies it, and misalignment if the brand promise still implies high customization.

**A9.**

MFP = $8,000 ÷ ($2,400 + $2,800 + $1,200) = $8,000 ÷ $6,400 = **1.25**

**A11.** While the delay appears in the **Finance department** lane (issuing the purchase order), the responsibility could trace back to any of three departments:

**Finance** — may not have processed the approved requisition promptly, delayed generating the PO document, or had an internal backlog.

**Procurement** — the purchase requisition submitted for approval may have been incomplete, incorrectly filled out, or missing required vendor information, causing Finance to pause or return it. Additionally, if vendor selection or quote gathering took too long, it compressed the timeline before Finance even received anything.

**Employee** — the original request may have been missing details or submitted incorrectly, forcing the process to restart, which cascades downstream and puts time pressure on every subsequent step including PO issuance.

**The swim lane insight:** The delay shows up in Finance's lane, but the root cause likely lives in a handoff — either from Procurement to Finance (incomplete requisition) or further upstream. This is exactly why swim lane diagrams are valuable: they reveal that most delays don't happen _inside_ a department, they happen _between_ them.
