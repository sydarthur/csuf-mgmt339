### **From SCM to Supply Chain Design**

    - **SCM:** Focuses on _coordinating_ partners and flows.
    - **SC Design:** Focuses on _architecting_ the structure of the entire network.
- **Key Questions**
    - What questions does design answer? (What should the supply chain look like? What trade-offs should we make?)
- **Impact**    
    - What do design choices determine? (Cost, speed, flexibility, and resilience).

- **Competitive Advantage**
    - How does design affect competition? (It determines how firms compete in cost, speed, sustainability, and service).
        
- **Outcomes**
    - **Poor Design:** Leads to delays, excess cost, or fragility during disruptions.
    - **Good Design:** Aligns with customer needs and company strategy.
    
### **Measuring Supply Chain Performance**

- **Definition of SCM**
    - It is the art and science of integrating flows from the "supplier’s supplier to the customer’s customer."
        
- **Common Metrics**
    - What are the key metrics? (Cost, quality, delivery speed, flexibility, and sustainability).
        
- **Frameworks**
    - **SCOR Model:** What processes does it evaluate? (Plan, Source, Make, Deliver, Return).
        

### **SC Design & Financial Performance**

- **Financial Health**
    - Does SC design only affect operations? (No, it affects the entire financial health of the firm).
        
- **Financial Levers**
    - **Revenue:** How is it increased? (Better customer service).
    - **Net Income:** How is it increased? (Lower transportation, purchasing, and overhead costs).
    - **Cash Flow:** How is it improved? (Faster flow reduces cash tied up in backlogs).
    - **ROA (Return on Assets):** How is it improved? (Higher net income + fewer total assets).
        

### **Inventory Measures for Supply Chain Design**

- **Key Measures**
    
    - **Average Aggregate Inventory Value:** Measures total investment in raw materials, WIP, and finished goods.
    - **Weeks of Supply:** Shows how long inventory can meet demand (Avg Inventory / Weekly Sales at Cost).
    - **Inventory Turnover:** Measures how many times inventory is sold/replaced per year (Annual Sales / Avg Inventory).
        
- **Efficiency**
    - What indicates a faster-moving supply chain? (Higher turnover and fewer weeks of supply).

### **Efficiency vs. Responsiveness**

- **The Trade-off**
    
    - **Efficient Supply Chains:** Focus on low cost, predictable demand, and high utilization.
    - **Responsive Supply Chains:** Focus on speed, flexibility, and the ability to handle uncertainty.
- **Decision:** Firms must choose which dimension matters most for their customers.
    

### **Customer-Centric SC Strategies**

- **Segmentation**
    
    - Should all customers get the same supply chain? (No, different segments may require different models).
    - **Example:** Nike balances mass retail shipments (Efficiency) with responsive custom orders (Responsiveness).
        

### **Global vs. Regional Network Design**

- **Global Networks:** Leverage scale and low-cost regions.
- **Regional Networks:** Reduce lead time, transportation cost, and risk.
- **Trends:** A shift toward "nearshoring" (e.g., in North America) to increase resilience.

- **Importance:** Location locks in cost and service performance for years.
- **Factors:** Labor markets, proximity to customers/suppliers, trade policies, and risk.

- **Ton Mile Center Problem (Center of Gravity)**
    
    - **Goal:** Find the (x, y) location that minimizes total transportation costs.
    - **The Logic:** Locations with higher **volume** and higher **shipping rates** pull the facility closer to them.
    - **Calculation:** It is a weighted average of coordinates based on (Volume × Rate).
        

### **Capacity and Network Configuration**

- **Facility Count Trade-off**
    - **More Facilities:** Increases responsiveness but raises costs and complexity.
    - **Fewer Facilities:** Lower cost, but may increase risk and lead time.        


### **Outsourcing Decisions**

- **Make vs. Buy**
    - **Core Competencies:** Should remain in-house.
    - **Non-Core Activities:** Can be outsourced to reduce cost and increase flexibility.
    - **Example:** Apple outsources manufacturing but keeps design/software in-house.


### **When to Use 3PLs and 4PLs**

- **3PL (Third-Party Logistics):** Handle specific services like transport and warehousing.
- **4PL (Fourth-Party Logistics):** Manage the _entire_ logistics network on behalf of the company.


### **Supply Chain Disruptions & Risk**

- **Disruptions:** Natural disasters, pandemics, strikes, cyberattacks.
- **Vulnerability:** Global chains are more exposed due to distance and interdependence.
- **Mitigation Strategies:**
    - **Dual Sourcing:** Protects against supplier failure.
    - **Regionalization:** Reduces transportation risk.
    - **Buffers:** Using safety stock or capacity to absorb shock.

### **Technology in Supply Chain Design**

- **ERP:** Integrates data across sourcing, production, and logistics.
- **AI:** Improves forecasting and disruption prediction.
- **Blockchain:** Enhances traceability and reduces fraud (e.g., Maersk).
- **Digital Twins:** Virtual replicas of the supply chain used to simulate disruptions and test designs.

### **Sustainability in Supply Chain Design**

- **Green Design:** Includes renewable energy, recyclable packaging, and ethical sourcing.
- **Drivers:** Customer expectations and regulations.
- **Example:** Unilever redesigns packaging to minimize waste.


### **Pulling It All Together**

- **Integration:** A well-designed chain balances efficiency, responsiveness, and resilience.
- **Evolution:** Design must change as markets and risks change.

### Ton-mile Center - Solved Problem

#### Key Concepts

- **Goal:** Find the (x, y) location that minimizes total transportation costs.
- **The Logic:** Locations with higher volume and higher shipping rates pull the facility closer to them.
- **Calculation:** It is a weighted average of coordinates based on (Volume × Rate)

#### Steps

1. **Identify coordinates and shipment data** for each location (volume and transportation rate)
2. **Calculate the weight** for each location: $w_i = V_i \times R_i$
3. **Multiply each weight by its x and y coordinates**: $w_i x_i$ and $w_i y_i$
4. **Sum all weights**: $\sum w_i$
5. **Calculate optimal x and y coordinates using the weighted average formula:**

$$x^* = \frac{\sum w_i x_i}{\sum w_i}, \quad y^* = \frac{\sum w_i y_i}{\sum w_i}$$

6. **Interpret the result**: The facility should be located at $(x^*, x^*)$

#### Example Problem

**ABC Electronics** needs to locate a new distribution center to serve three retail stores.

|Store|Location (x, y)|Weekly Shipments|Transport Rate|
|---|---|---|---|
|Store A|(10, 20)|400 boxes/week|$2.00/box-mile|
|Store B|(50, 10)|300 boxes/week|$2.00/box-mile|
|Store C|(30, 40)|500 boxes/week|$2.00/box-mile|

**Where should the distribution center be located to minimize total transportation costs?**

---



#### Step-by-Step Solution

##### Step 1 & 2: Calculate Weights $(w_i = V_i \times R_i)$

| Store     | Volume $(V_i)$ | Rate $(R_i)$ | Weight $(w_i)$ |
| --------- | -------------- | ------------ | -------------- |
| Store A   | 400            | $2.00        | 800            |
| Store B   | 300            | $2.00        | 600            |
| Store C   | 500            | $2.00        | 1,000          |
| **Total** |                |              | **2,400**      |



##### Step 3: Multiply Weights by Coordinates

|Store|$w_i$|$x_i$|$y_i$|$w_i x_i$|$w_i y_i$|
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
|Store A|800|10|20|8,000|16,000|
|Store B|600|50|10|30,000|6,000|
|Store C|1,000|30|40|30,000|40,000|
|**Total**|**2,400**|||**68,000**|**62,000**|

##### Steps 4 & 5: Calculate Optimal Coordinates

|Coordinate|Formula|Calculation|Result|
|---|---|---|---|
|$x^*$|$\dfrac{\sum w_i x_i}{\sum w_i}$|$\dfrac{68,000}{2,400}$|**28.33**|
|$y^*$|$\dfrac{\sum w_i y_i}{\sum w_i}$|$\dfrac{62,000}{2,400}$|**25.83**|

##### Step 6: Result

 **The distribution center should be located at approximately (28.3, 25.8)**
 
> This point balances the weighted pulls from all three stores, minimizing total transportation cost. Notice the facility is pulled closer to **Store C** since it has the highest volume (500 boxes/week).

### Inventory Performance Metrics - Solved Problem

#### Key Concepts
- **Goal:** Measure how efficiently a company manages its inventory.
- **The Logic:** Inventory tied up in raw materials, WIP, and finished goods represents cash that is not being used productively. These metrics help evaluate inventory efficiency.
- **Key Insight:** Higher inventory turnover and lower weeks of supply generally indicate leaner, more efficient operations.


#### Key Formulas

| Metric | Formula |
|--------|---------|
| Average Aggregate Inventory Value | $\sum (\text{Units}_i \times \text{Cost}_i)$ |
| Average Weekly Sales | $\dfrac{\text{Annual COGS}}{\text{Weeks per Year}}$ |
| Weeks of Supply | $\dfrac{\text{Average Aggregate Inventory Value}}{\text{Average Weekly Sales}}$ |
| Inventory Turnover | $\dfrac{\text{Annual COGS}}{\text{Average Aggregate Inventory Value}}$ |


#### Steps
1. **Calculate the value of each inventory type**: Units × Cost per Unit
2. **Sum all inventory values** to get Average Aggregate Inventory Value
3. **Calculate Average Weekly Sales**: Annual Cost of Goods Sold (COGS) ÷ 52 weeks
4. **Calculate Weeks of Supply**: Aggregate Inventory Value ÷ Average Weekly Sales
5. **Calculate Inventory Turnover**: Annual COGS ÷ Aggregate Inventory Value
6. **Interpret the result**: Higher turnover = leaner inventory; Lower weeks of supply = faster moving stock

---

#### Example Problem

A company operates 52 weeks per year and has an annual COGS of $5,200,000.
The company carries three types of inventory:

| Inventory Type | Units | Cost per Unit |
|----------------|-------|---------------|
| Raw Materials (RM) | 500 | $20 |
| Work in Process (WIP) | 200 | $50 |
| Finished Goods (FG) | 100 | $100 |

**Find: Average Aggregate Inventory Value, Average Weekly Sales, Weeks of Supply, and Inventory Turnover.**


#### Step-by-Step Solution

##### Step 1 & 2: Calculate Average Aggregate Inventory Value

| Inventory Type | Units | Cost per Unit | Inventory Value |
|----------------|-------|---------------|-----------------|
| Raw Materials (RM) | 500 | $20 | $10,000 |
| Work in Process (WIP) | 200 | $50 | $10,000 |
| Finished Goods (FG) | 100 | $100 | $10,000 |
| **Total** | | | **$30,000** |



##### Step 3: Calculate Average Weekly Sales

| Item | Value |
|------|-------|
| Annual COGS | $5,200,000 |
| Weeks per Year | 52 |
| **Average Weekly Sales** | $\dfrac{\$5,200,000}{52} =$ **$100,000/week** |



##### Step 4: Calculate Weeks of Supply

| Item | Value |
|------|-------|
| Average Aggregate Inventory Value | $30,000 |
| Average Weekly Sales | $100,000 |
| **Weeks of Supply** | $\dfrac{\$30,000}{\$100,000} =$ **0.3 weeks** |



##### Step 5: Calculate Inventory Turnover

| Item | Value |
|------|-------|
| Annual COGS | $5,200,000 |
| Average Aggregate Inventory Value | $30,000 |
| **Inventory Turnover** | $\dfrac{\$5,200,000}{\$30,000} =$ **173.33 turns/year** |



##### Step 6: Summary and Interpretation

| Metric | Value | Interpretation |
|--------|-------|----------------|
| Average Aggregate Inventory Value | $30,000 | Total cash tied up in inventory |
| Average Weekly Sales | $100,000/week | COGS moving through per week |
| Weeks of Supply | 0.3 weeks (~2 days) | Very lean - only 2 days of stock on hand |
| Inventory Turnover | 173.33 turns/year | Inventory cycles extremely fast |

**Key Takeaway** 

> This company operates with very lean inventory, turning stock over 173 times per year and holding only about 2 days worth of supply. While efficient, this also means the company is vulnerable to supply disruptions with very little buffer stock.
