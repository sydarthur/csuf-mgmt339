Economic Order Quantity
MGMT 339
Inventory Management

How much should
you order?

Order too little — you're constantly reordering, burning time on paperwork. Order too much — you're drowning in carrying costs. There's a sweet spot between these two forces. This walkthrough is about finding it, understanding why it exists, and knowing when it doesn't apply.

The Core Intuition

Two forces pulling against each other

Before any math, picture a warehouse manager making one decision: how big should each order be?

Two completely different people yell different things at her:

The Accountant
"Order small batches!"
Inventory sitting on shelves is cash frozen in place. It incurs capital costs, storage costs, insurance, and risk of obsolescence. The less you hold, the less it costs.
The Purchasing Manager
"Order large batches!"
Every order has fixed overhead — paperwork, receiving, shipping fees, inspection. Placing 50 orders a year is a logistical nightmare. Place fewer, larger orders.

Both are right. The accountant wants to minimize holding cost. The purchasing manager wants to minimize ordering cost. These costs pull in opposite directions as Q changes. The EOQ is where their tug-of-war settles.

The Tug of War
What We're Solving
Find the order quantity Q* that minimizes the sum of holding cost and ordering cost. We'll build each piece, then combine.
The First Cost

Holding inventory isn't free

Every unit sitting in your warehouse is costing you money right now. This is called the Inventory Carrying Cost (ICC) — and students consistently underestimate how large it is.

Take an inkjet printer worth $100. Here's what it actually costs to hold one unit for a year:

Holding Cost per Year
ICC/yr = (Q/2) × H
Q/2 = average inventory (starts at Q, drains to 0, averages Q/2) · H = holding cost per unit per year
Why Average Inventory = Q/2
When an order of Q arrives, you have Q units. You consume them linearly until 0, then reorder. Over the cycle, your average on-hand is (Q + 0)/2 = Q/2. This is the triangle under the sawtooth — we'll see it next.
The Second Cost

Every order costs money to place

Ordering isn't free either. Every purchase order triggers a chain of work: someone analyzes inventory, creates the PO, coordinates with the vendor, receives the shipment, inspects it, and processes payment. Whether you order 10 units or 10,000, that chain of work is largely fixed.

📋
Ordering Costs (S) PO creation, systems costs, receiving labor, vendor payment processing, inventory analysis, freight fixed fees. Incurred per order placed.
🔧
Setup Costs (S) For manufacturing: changeover time, tooling adjustment, calibration, and scrap from the first run. Incurred per production batch.

Here's the key insight that drives the whole problem: order quantity (Q) and order frequency move in opposite directions. If annual demand is fixed at D, then ordering Q each time means D/Q orders per year.

The Tradeoff
Order larger Q: fewer orders per year → ordering cost falls. But average inventory (Q/2) is higher → holding cost rises.

Order smaller Q: more orders per year → ordering cost rises. But average inventory is lower → holding cost falls.

Neither extreme is optimal. The question is: where's the balance?
Ordering Cost per Year
Ordering Cost/yr = (D/Q) × S
D/Q = orders per year · S = cost per order

The Core Insight

The costs pull against each other

Let's plot both costs against Q on the same graph. Carrying cost rises linearly with Q (bigger batches → more inventory to hold). Ordering cost falls as 1/Q (bigger batches → fewer orders). Their sum — Total Annual Cost (TAC) — forms a U-shaped curve. The minimum of that curve is the EOQ.

The Formula

Solving for the optimal Q

To find the minimum of TAC, we take the derivative with respect to Q and set it to zero. The result is remarkably clean:

Economic Order Quantity
EOQ = √( 2DS / H )
D = Annual demand (units/yr)  |  S = Cost per order ($/order)
H = Holding cost ($/unit/yr) = unit cost × ICC rate

Why a square root?

The square root isn't arbitrary. It tells you something important about how EOQ responds to changes. Doubling any input doesn't double the output — it only multiplies it by √2 ≈ 1.414.

Sensitivity — EOQ scales with √change

Dampened Response
The √ function dampens large shocks. A 4× surge in demand only prescribes a 2× larger order. This makes EOQ decisions remarkably stable — small errors in estimating D, S, or H don't swing the recommended order size wildly. That's a feature, not a bug.
📈
D or S goes up → EOQ increases. Demand is high or ordering is expensive, so batch larger to spread the fixed cost.
📦
H goes up → EOQ decreases. Holding is expensive, so carry less inventory at a time, even if you reorder more.
Assumptions Baked Into EOQ
  • Demand is continuous, constant, and known
  • Lead time is constant and known
  • All demand is satisfied — no stockouts allowed
  • Unit price is constant (no quantity discounts)
  • Only carrying cost and ordering cost matter (not purchase price)
Every assumption is violated somewhere in the real world. We'll cover what to do in those cases later.

A Practical Bonus

The cost curve has a flat bottom

Here's a subtle but incredibly useful property of the EOQ: the TAC curve is surprisingly flat near the minimum. Being slightly off the optimal Q costs almost nothing.

This matters because in real life, you'll rarely order exactly 367.42 units. You'll order 350 (half a case) or 400 (a full case), or whatever your supplier's minimum lot size happens to be. The good news: you don't pay much for rounding.

What This Means in Practice
Being within ±20% of the EOQ costs you less than 2% extra. That's a huge operational win. Round up to a full pallet, match the supplier's minimum, align with a shipping container — all without paying much penalty. EOQ is a target, not a prescription.
The Catch — Asymmetry
Notice the table isn't perfectly symmetric. Errors on the low side (too small Q) are slightly more forgiving than errors on the high side once you get far out. But within ±30%, the penalty is tiny in either direction.
When to Reorder

The Continuous Review System

EOQ tells you how much to order. But you also need to know when. The continuous review system (also called a fixed-Q system) watches inventory in real time and triggers an order when stock drops to a threshold called the Reorder Point (R).

Inventory with Reorder Point — Animated

Each time inventory hits R, a fixed order of Q = 367 units is placed. The order arrives exactly one lead time later, replenishing stock back to Q.

Reorder Point (Constant Demand)
R = d × L
d = daily demand rate, L = lead time in days
Hospital Example
Daily demand = 10,800 ÷ 360 = 30 units/day
Lead time = 6 days
R = 30 × 6 = 180 units

When stock drops to 180 units, place an order for 367 units. The 180 units will last exactly 6 days — just in time for the new shipment.
Inventory Position (IP)
IP = On-Hand + Scheduled Receipts − Backorders

You track inventory position (not just on-hand) when checking against R, because an order may already be in transit. This prevents double-ordering.
When Demand Varies

Safety stock protects against uncertainty

The simple model assumes perfectly constant demand. In reality, demand fluctuates. If demand spikes during lead time, you can run out before your order arrives. Safety stock is a buffer against this. The higher your service level target, the more safety stock you need.

Reorder Point with Variable Demand
Reorder Point with Safety Stock
R = d̄·L + z·σdL
d̄ = avg daily demand  |  L = lead time
z = service level z-score  |  σdL = std dev of demand during lead time
How to Find Safety Stock
1. Choose a service level (e.g., 95% → z = 1.65)
2. Estimate the standard deviation of demand during lead time
3. Safety Stock = z × σdL

Higher service level = more safety stock = more carrying cost. It's always a tradeoff between stockout risk and holding cost.

Diminishing Returns
Going from 95% to 99% service roughly doubles safety stock in most cases. Going from 99% to 99.9% doubles it again. The extra reliability gets expensive fast. This is why healthcare and defense tolerate costs that retail doesn't.
Limits of the Model

When EOQ doesn't apply

EOQ is a remarkably useful framework, but every assumption can break. Recognizing when is half the skill.

Quantity Discounts
If a supplier offers a price break at Q = 500 (say, 3% off), the purchase cost suddenly jumps into the analysis. The EOQ formula ignores purchase price, but with discounts the savings may outweigh the extra holding cost. Fix: compute TAC + purchase cost at the EOQ and at each discount threshold; pick the lowest.
Lumpy / Intermittent Demand
EOQ assumes demand is steady. If your SKU sells 50 units one week and zero for three weeks, the "average" misleads you. The sawtooth isn't really a sawtooth. Fix: use MRP, Silver-Meal heuristics, or period-order-quantity approaches designed for discrete demand.
The JIT / Lean Philosophy
Lean thinking flips the premise: rather than accepting S as fixed, attack S itself. Reduce setup times via SMED, partner tightly with suppliers, use kanban signals. As S approaches zero, EOQ approaches zero — and you've enabled single-piece flow.

This is one of Toyota's central insights: EOQ gives you the best answer given your current S, but S isn't fixed by physics.
Perishables & Obsolescence Risk
For products with a shelf life (food, pharmaceuticals) or fast obsolescence (electronics, fashion), holding costs aren't just interest and storage — they're the risk of writing off the entire batch. H should be inflated dramatically, which drives EOQ down. Some industries replace EOQ entirely with newsvendor-model thinking.
Capacity Constraints
If the EOQ says "order 10,000 units" but your warehouse holds 6,000, the model is giving advice you can't follow. Constrained EOQ problems require a Lagrangian approach — or, practically, just order up to capacity and accept the higher total cost.
Summary

The complete picture

The EOQ framework answers two fundamental inventory questions: how much to order, and when to order. Here are all the pieces in one place.

Holding Cost per Year
ICC/yr = (Q/2) × H
Ordering Cost per Year
OC/yr = (D/Q) × S
Total Annual Cost
TAC = (Q/2)H + (D/Q)S
EOQ Formula
EOQ = √(2DS/H)
Reorder Point (fixed demand)
R = d × L
Reorder Point (variable demand)
R = d̄·L + z·σdL

Five big takeaways

⚖️
Two costs in tension. Holding cost rises with Q, ordering cost falls with Q. EOQ sits where they balance.
√
Square-root dampening. Doubling D or S only multiplies EOQ by 1.41. This makes EOQ stable to estimation errors.
📏
Flat bottom. Being within ±20% of EOQ costs less than 2%. Round up to pallet, case, or container without worry.
🔔
Safety stock is for uncertainty. Variable demand or lead times require a buffer — and higher service levels get expensive fast.
🚫
Know when it breaks. Quantity discounts, perishables, lumpy demand, capacity limits, and Lean philosophy all demand different tools.
