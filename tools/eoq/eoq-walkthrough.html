How much should
you order?

Order too little and you're constantly reordering — burning time and money on paperwork. Order too much and you're drowning in carrying costs. There's a sweet spot. This is how you find it.

The First Cost

Holding inventory isn't free

Every unit sitting in your warehouse is costing you money right now. This is called the Inventory Carrying Cost (ICC) — and it adds up fast.

Take an inkjet printer worth $100. Here's what it actually costs to hold one unit for a year:

ICC Breakdown — Inkjet Printer ($100/unit)
Cost ComponentHow It's CalculatedRate
Capital (opportunity cost) Money tied up in inventory can't earn returns elsewhere 25.0%
Storage space $12/pallet/mo to store + $10 in + $10 out → $3.20/unit 3.2%
Inventory service Taxes (1.5%) + Insurance (0.5%) 2.0%
Inventory risk Historical shrinkage, obsolescence, damage 1.0%
Total ICC Rate (H) 31.2% × $100/unit = $31.20/unit/year 31.2%
Bottom Line
It costs $31.20/year to hold a single $100 printer. If your average inventory is 2,500 units, that's $78,000/year just to keep them on the shelf.
ICC per Year
ICC/yr = (Q/2) × H
where Q/2 = average inventory, H = holding cost per unit per year
The Second Cost

Every order costs money to place

Ordering isn't free either. Every purchase order triggers a chain of work: someone analyzes inventory, creates the PO, coordinates with the vendor, receives the shipment, and processes payment.

📋
Ordering Costs (S) PO creation, systems costs, receiving, vendor payment, inventory analysis. Fixed cost per order placed.
🔧
Setup Costs (S) For manufacturing: changeover time when production switches from one product to another.

The key insight: order quantity (Q) and order frequency move in opposite directions.

The Tradeoff
Order more at once (larger Q): fewer orders per year → lower ordering cost. But average inventory is higher → higher carrying cost.

Order less at once (smaller Q): more orders per year → higher ordering cost. But average inventory is lower → lower carrying cost.
Order Cost per Year
Order Cost/yr = (D/Q) × S
where D/Q = number of orders per year, S = cost per order
The Core Insight

The costs pull in opposite directions

As order quantity Q increases, carrying cost rises and ordering cost falls. Their sum — Total Annual Cost — forms a U-shaped curve with a flat bottom. The minimum of that curve is the EOQ.

Total Annual Cost Curve
Total Annual Cost (TAC)
TAC = (Q/2)·H  +  (D/Q)·S
Carrying cost term  +  Ordering cost term
Key Property
At the EOQ, carrying cost equals ordering cost. This isn't a coincidence — it's a mathematical consequence of minimizing their sum.
The Formula

Solving for the optimal Q

To find the minimum of TAC, take the derivative with respect to Q and set it to zero. The result is remarkably clean:

Economic Order Quantity
EOQ = √( 2DS / H )
D = Annual demand (units/yr)  |  S = Cost per order ($/order)
H = Holding cost ($/unit/yr) = unit cost × ICC rate

The logic: a larger D means you need bigger batches to spread ordering cost. A larger S means ordering is expensive, so order more each time. A larger H means holding is expensive, so order less each time.

📈
D or S goes up → EOQ increases. Demand is high or ordering is expensive, so batch larger.
📦
H goes up → EOQ decreases. Holding is expensive, so carry less inventory at a time.
Assumptions of the Simple EOQ Model
  • Demand is continuous, constant, and known
  • Lead time is constant and known
  • All demand is satisfied (no stockouts)
  • Price per unit is constant (no quantity discounts)
  • Only carrying cost and ordering cost are considered
Worked Example

Let's calculate the actual numbers

St. John's Hospital needs to order a medical supply. Here are the parameters:

Given Information
Annual Demand (D)10,800 units/yr
Unit Cost (C)$160/unit
Holding Cost Rate25%
H = 25% × $160$40/unit/yr
Ordering Cost (S)$250/order
Lead Time (L)6 days
Step 1: Calculate EOQ
EOQ = √( 2 × 10,800 × 250 / 40 )
     = √( 5,400,000 / 40 )
     = √135,000
≈ 367 units
Step 2: Verify Costs Balance at EOQ
Carrying cost  = (367/2) × $40    = $7,340/yr
Ordering cost = (10,800/367) × $250 = $7,357/yr
(rounding difference — they're equal at the true EOQ ✓)

TAC          = $7,340 + $7,357      = $14,697/yr
When to Reorder

The Continuous Review System

EOQ tells you how much to order. But you also need to know when. The continuous review system (also called a fixed-Q system) watches your inventory in real time and triggers an order when it drops to a threshold called the Reorder Point (R).

Inventory Level Over Time — Sawtooth Pattern

Each time inventory hits R, a fixed order of Q = 367 units is placed. The order arrives exactly one lead time later, replenishing stock back to Q.

Reorder Point (Constant Demand)
R = d × L
d = daily demand rate, L = lead time in days
Hospital Example
Daily demand = 10,800 ÷ 360 = 30 units/day
Lead time = 6 days
R = 30 × 6 = 180 units

When stock drops to 180 units, place an order for 367 units.
Inventory Position (IP)
IP = On-Hand + Scheduled Receipts − Backorders

You track IP (not just on-hand) because an order may already be in transit. This prevents double-ordering.
When Demand Varies

Safety stock protects against uncertainty

The simple model assumes perfectly constant demand. In reality, demand fluctuates. If demand spikes during lead time, you can run out before your order arrives. Safety stock is a buffer against this.

Reorder Point with Variable Demand
Reorder Point with Safety Stock
R = d̄·L + z·σdL
d̄ = avg daily demand  |  L = lead time
z = service level z-score  |  σdL = std dev of demand during lead time
How to Find Safety Stock
1. Choose a service level (e.g., 95% → z = 1.65)
2. Identify the demand distribution during lead time
3. Safety Stock = z × σdL

Higher service level = more safety stock = more carrying cost. It's a tradeoff.
Summary

The complete picture

The EOQ framework answers two fundamental inventory questions: how much to order, and when to order.

Holding Cost per Year
ICC/yr = (Q/2) × H
Ordering Cost per Year
OC/yr = (D/Q) × S
Total Annual Cost
TAC = (Q/2)H + (D/Q)S
EOQ Formula
EOQ = √(2DS/H)
Reorder Point (fixed demand)
R = d × L
Reorder Point (variable demand)
R = d̄·L + z·σdL
Hospital Example — Final Answer
D = 10,800  |  H = $40  |  S = $250  |  L = 6 days

Order 367 units each time inventory drops to 180 units.
