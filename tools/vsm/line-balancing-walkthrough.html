The Problem

A mixer assembly line
9 tasks, one goal

A factory assembles stand mixers. There are 9 work elements — each with a set time and constraints about which tasks must come first. We need to assign them to workstations to meet a target output rate.

Task Description Time (sec) Predecessor(s)
ABolt leg frame to hopper40—
BInsert impeller shaft30A
CAttach axle50A
DAttach agitator40B
EAttach drive wheel6B
FAttach free wheel25C
GMount lower post15C
HAttach controls20D, E
IMount nameplate18F, G
Total244
Target Output
2,440 units/week → 60 units/hour
The factory runs 40.67 hours per week. Desired output = 2,440 ÷ 40.67 ≈ 60 units per hour. This is our production rate r.
Step 1

Calculate the
Cycle Time

The cycle time is the maximum time allowed at any workstation. It's the reciprocal of the output rate — how many seconds we have between finished units rolling off the line.

Cycle Time Formula
c = 1 / r
= 1 / (60 units/hr) = 1/60 hr/unit = 60 seconds/unit
Every 60 seconds, one finished mixer must leave the line. No single station can take longer than 60 seconds — or the whole line falls behind.

Step 2

Draw the
Precedence Diagram

Before assigning tasks to stations, we map out the dependencies. Tasks can only start after their predecessors are complete — this diagram shows the required order.

Notice the two parallel paths: A → B → {D,E} → H and A → C → {F,G} → I. Tasks on different paths have no dependency on each other and can potentially be combined into one station.

Step 3

Calculate the
Theoretical Minimum

Before we assign tasks, we calculate the fewest stations we could possibly need. This gives a lower bound — reality may require more due to precedence constraints.

Theoretical Minimum Stations
TM = ⌈ Total Task Time / Cycle Time ⌉
= ⌈ 244 / 60 ⌉ = ⌈ 4.067 ⌉ = 5 stations
What does TM mean?
A floor, not a guarantee
TM = 5 means we need at least 5 stations. We can't do it in 4 because there isn't enough time per station (244 ÷ 4 = 61s > 60s limit). But precedence constraints could force us to use 6 or more — we won't know until we actually assign the tasks.
If TM equals our actual number of stations, we got lucky. Usually precedence constraints force a few idle seconds somewhere.
Step 4

Assign tasks using the
Largest Candidate Rule

The Largest Candidate Rule (LCR) is a simple heuristic: at each station, assign the largest eligible task first, then keep filling with the next-largest task that fits and is eligible, until nothing more fits. Repeat for the next station.

LCR Priority List
Tasks ranked by time (descending)
C(50) > A(40) = D(40) > B(30) > F(25) > H(20) > I(18) > G(15) > E(6)

A task is eligible when all its predecessors have already been assigned to earlier stations. We only consider eligible tasks when filling a station.

Step 6

Calculate
Line Efficiency

Even with a balanced line, there's always some idle time. Efficiency measures how much of the total station capacity is actually used doing productive work.

Line Efficiency Formula
E = Total Task Time / (N × c)
= 244 / (5 × 60) = 244 / 300 = 81.3%
Line Efficiency
81.3%
244 productive seconds out of 300 total station-seconds per cycle
Idle time = 300 − 244 = 56 seconds wasted per cycle
Total Station-Seconds
5 × 60 = 300s
If every station ran at full capacity every cycle — the theoretical maximum.
Idle Time Breakdown
56s per cycle
S1: 20s · S2: 10s · S3: 5s · S4: 5s · S5: 16s = 56s total idle
Is 81.3% good?
It hits the theoretical minimum — that's hard to beat
We achieved TM = 5 stations, which means the precedence constraints didn't force us to add a 6th. An efficiency above 80% with 5 stations is typical for balanced lines. The remaining 18.7% idle time is largely unavoidable given the task structure — it's the cost of precedence constraints, not poor planning.

Line balancing is a constrained optimization problem. The LCR heuristic doesn't always find the optimal solution, but it's fast, systematic, and usually gets you close.
