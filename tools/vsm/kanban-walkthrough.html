Kanban Systems
MGMT 339
From Push to Pull

What if the factory
only made things
when asked?

In a push system, every station works as fast as it can and shoves output to the next step — whether it's ready or not. The result? Mountains of WIP.

A Kanban system flips this. The downstream step sends a signal — a card — back upstream that says: "I just used some. Send me more."

Nothing is produced until that signal arrives. No signal, no production.

The Key Insight
Baking never starts a new batch until it receives a Kanban card from Packaging. No card = no production. This is what makes it a pull system — demand pulls product through the line, rather than supply pushing it.
But how many containers should be circulating in this loop? Too few and Packaging starves. Too many and we're back to a push system with mountains of WIP. There's a formula for that.
The Math

The Kanban Formula

This formula tells you exactly how many containers need to circulate in the loop. Not one more, not one less.

k = d ( v + r ) ( 1 + a ) / c
k = number of Kanban containers
d = daily demand (units/day)
v = waiting / handling time (days)
r = processing time per container (days)
a = safety factor (e.g., 0.10 = 10%)
c = units per container

Let's make these variables come alive with a story.

The Story

A Coffee Shop's
Custom Printed Cups

Imagine you run a coffee shop. You order custom printed cups from a supplier. They arrive in plastic tote bins. When a bin is empty, you send it back and they refill it. This is a Kanban loop.

Let's figure out how many bins need to be circulating.

d
Daily Demand
How thirsty are the customers? How many cups do we burn through in a day?
500 cups/day
v
Waiting / Handling Time
When we send the empty bin back, how long does it sit on the truck or wait on the loading dock?
0.1 days
r
Processing Time
How long does it take the supplier to actually print and fill the bin with new cups?
0.2 days
a
Safety Factor
Reality is messy. Trucks get flat tires. Demand spikes randomly. We add a buffer.
0.10 (10%)
c
Units per Container
How many cups actually fit inside one plastic tote bin?
50 cups/bin
Key Insight: v + r
If you add waiting time and processing time together, that is your total Lead Time. It's the total time you are blind — between sending the empty bin out and getting a full one back. In our case: 0.1 + 0.2 = 0.3 days.
The Math in Action

Plug it in,
solve it.

k = 500 × (0.1 + 0.2) × (1 + 0.10) / 50
①
v + r = 0.1 + 0.2 = 0.3 days
Total lead time
②
d × (v + r) = 500 × 0.3 = 150 cups
Demand during lead time
③
1 + a = 1 + 0.10 = 1.10
Safety multiplier
④
150 × 1.10 = 165 cups
Demand + safety buffer
⑤
165 / 50 = 3.3
Divide by container size
Answer
k ≈ 3.3 → round up to 4 bins
4 plastic tote bins need to circulate between the coffee shop and the supplier to ensure you never run out of cups — but also never have a cluttered backroom.
"The Kanban formula doesn't tell you how to run faster. It tells you exactly how much inventory you need to absorb the gap between demand and replenishment — no more, no less."

The formula is simple. The thinking behind it is powerful.

Kanban forces you to define your lead time, acknowledge your uncertainty, and size your buffers deliberately — instead of just piling up inventory "just in case."
