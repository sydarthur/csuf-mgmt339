Every system has

We've learned how Lean thinking synchronizes a system to eliminate waste. But what happens when you ask: what actually limits this system's output?

Let's walk through a simple burger shop to see how Lean thinking naturally leads to the Theory of Constraints.

Three workers,
one burger line

The Push Problem

Everyone works hard.
The system fails.

In a typical operation, each worker tries to maximize their own output. Ashley can prep 5 buns, so she does all 5 and pushes them to Bailey.

Ashley made 5, but Bailey can only grill 3. Two buns pile up. Cesar only gets 3 burgers to wrap even though he can handle 4. And every 10 minutes, the pile grows.

"Optimizing each worker individually does not optimize the entire system."

WIP piles up. Space gets cluttered. Lead time increases. Customers wait longer. Everyone is working hard, but the system performs poorly.

Lean Thinking

Match the pace of
the slowest step

Lean asks a different question: What pace should the entire system operate at?

We look at the capacities and find the slowest step:

📊 CAPACITY COMPARISON
Ashley
5 burgers
Goal: 4
Bailey
3 burgers
Cesar
4 burgers
← Bailey is the constraint. The system can only move at 3 burgers / 10 min.
Lean Solution
Instead of pushing, we synchronize. Ashley prepares only 3 buns. Bailey grills 3. Cesar wraps 3. No WIP piles up. Smooth flow. This is a pull system — production matches the pace of the constraint.
System Output
3 burgers / 10 min
Smooth flow, but below the goal of 4. Can we do better?
Theory of Constraints

What limits
the system's output?

Lean helped us synchronize and eliminate WIP. But the output is still only 3 burgers. The entire shop can't produce more than 3 every 10 minutes because of one station.

TOC says: every system has one primary constraint. Improvement should focus only on that constraint.

🎯 TOC — THE FIVE FOCUSING STEPS
1
Identify the Constraint
Bailey's grill station — capacity of 3 vs. goal of 4.
2
Exploit the Constraint
Make sure the grill is never idle. Ashley keeps buns ready. Cesar clears finished burgers immediately. Zero interruptions at the grill.
3
Subordinate Everything Else
Ashley matches the grill pace. No point prepping 5 buns when only 3 can be grilled. Other stations serve the constraint.
4
Elevate the Constraint
Increase grill capacity: better equipment, two patties at once, preheating, or adding another grill.
5
Repeat — Don't Let Inertia Set In
Once the constraint is broken, go back to Step 1. A new constraint will emerge.
The Constraint Shifts

Fix one bottleneck,
another appears

Suppose we upgrade the grill. Bailey can now cook 5 burgers per 10 minutes.

What happens to the system?

Before
Ashley: 5
Bailey: 3 ← bottleneck
Cesar: 4
Output = 3
→
After Improvement
Ashley: 5
Bailey: 5 ✓ fixed
Cesar: 4 ← new bottleneck
Output = 4 ✓
The TOC Cycle
The constraint has shifted from Bailey to Cesar. We met our goal of 4 burgers. But if demand increases to 6? We go back to Step 1 and improve Cesar's station next. Continuous improvement is a cycle, not a one-time fix.
Find constraint → Improve → Find next constraint
IDENTIFY → EXPLOIT → SUBORDINATE → ELEVATE → REPEAT
The Key Distinction

Lean vs. TOC:
Two lenses, one goal

Students should walk away understanding the difference:

Lean
Flow & Waste
Smooth production flow. Minimize inventory. Pull-based system. Synchronize to the pace of demand.
Theory of Constraints
Throughput & Bottleneck
Identify the constraint. Improve the constraint. Increase system capacity. Repeat the cycle.
"Lean tells you how to run smooth. TOC tells you where to focus."

Think about it

If demand increases to 6 burgers every 10 minutes, what should we improve next? And what happens after that?
